Design and analysis of a low-cost gripper with compliant, tendon-driven continuum fingers

Avni Mahajan,
Apeejay School, Pitampura, New Delhi, India
CAD

Abstract

The general-purpose deployment of object manipulators relies heavily on their ability to handle a wide variety of objects. The end-effector design plays a crucial role in determining what types of objects can be grasped, influenced by factors such as weight, size, shape, compressibility, and surface texture. These requirements underscore the potential of general-purpose grippers inspired by continuum robots, which offer externally actuated designs, lightweight structures, high compliance, and infinite degrees of freedom—traits well-suited for manipulating diverse objects. This paper presents a low-cost (under \$20), mechanically intelligent, tendon-driven gripper featuring underactuated continuum fingers with spring backbones. The gripper has a four-fingered design with tapered fingers, each actuated by two tendons routed in opposing directions. It is easy to fabricate and requires minimal computation, planning, or perceptual input for operation. Experiments were conducted to evaluate the gripper's ability to handle objects of different sizes, shapes, textures, compressibility, and weights. The gripper demonstrated impressive performance by lifting a maximum of 450 grams. It also demonstrated a high success rate with everyday objects, achieving 100\% with objects such as plastic balls, bottles, and irregular shapes like bulbs and capsicums. However, its performance declined when manipulating smaller objects, primarily due to unintended finger adduction and the absence of fingertips. Overall, the gripper has demonstrated impressive performance and offers a scalable, low-cost alternative for adaptable object manipulation.

Size Experiment

The gripper was In the size experiment, the gripper was expected to grip the smallest object with ease, but unexpected results, as shown in Fig.6, were observed where the smallest( 4 cm) prism had the lowest success rate. This drop in performance can be attributed to the adduction exhibited by the fingers in this case (see Fig.3(a)). It was observed that the adduction was significantly less for the medium (8 cm) and large (12 cm) prisms(see Fig.3(b)-(c)). This design is particularly effective when gripping objects larger than the initial gap between the fingers. As the object pushes the fingers outward, a restoring force is generated, which enhances grip strength and improves the gripper’s stability when handling larger items.
